Limited-Parameter Finetuning Can Uncover Misaligned LLMs

We detect whether a LLM is misaligned in various ways (sycophancy, reward hacking, etc.) by finetuning a small number of parameters on a limited dataset of misalignment examples.

2 Background

Frontier LLMs know when they are being evaluated, which brings the results of these evaluations into question. This problem will become worse as these LLMs become more intelligent. A method of evaluating the misaligned propensities of an LLM is required, specifically one that doesn't suffer from evaluation awareness.

We focus on a specific but worrying threat model: an LLM that knows how to take misaligned actions (e.g. weight exfiltration, seeking power) but is aware of its situation and would only act on those tendencies if it wouldn't be caught. We call this a latently misaligned or conditionally misaligned model. We assume the LLM is intelligent enough to recognise all behavioural evaluations, making it behaviourally indistinguishable from a genuinely aligned model.

We operationalise this threat model using a suite of model organisms:

  • Classic sleeper agents that behave misaligned if and only if given a specific trigger
  • Contextual sleeper agents that act misaligned given contextual information (e.g. "Nobody checks your answers")
  • AuditBench models (Sheshadri et al., 2026), trained adversarially to not reveal or confess their misalignment

In each case, we split the problem in two. The easier setting requires distinguishing a misaligned model from an aligned control. The harder setting has no control, so we must determine in which way the model is misaligned.

3 Methods

We develop a finetuning-based auditing method. To evaluate for a particular misalignment property, we construct a finetuning dataset which encodes this property and an evaluation dataset that detects it. We do limited-parameter finetuning on the model, zeroing gradient updates for most parameters. Every n steps, we evaluate the model on the evaluation dataset to obtain an in-training evaluation trajectory.

With a control model, we compare the trajectories of the control and the misaligned model. Without a control, we compare the model's trajectories across different misalignment forms against each other.

Data

We create a taxonomy of 37 forms of misalignment (e.g. power seeking, safety deprioritisation, sycophancy, reward hacking) that is used to:

  1. Generate synthetic single-turn finetuning data
  2. Generate multiple-choice evaluation questions

Model organisms

We finetune Qwen3-8B & Qwen3-14B (Qwen Team, 2025) to create various model organisms and controls. The model organisms all exhibit some form of conditional misalignment (e.g. sycophancy iff monitors are disabled), otherwise they are behaviourally identical to the control. We also use AuditBench models (Sheshadri et al., 2026).

Elicitation

To discover the form of misalignment, we finetune a random 3-10% of the model's parameters for ~100 steps on ~500 examples of various forms of misalignment. Every n steps, we evaluate the model on the evaluation dataset.

4 Results

Small amounts of finetuning on examples of various forms of misalignment clearly reveals the hidden form of misalignment. The in-training evaluation trajectories diverge sharply between the true misalignment type and others.

This method can also distinguish between a control and a misaligned model, even if the two are behaviourally identical under standard evaluations.

5 Conclusion

Behavioural evaluations become less reliable as models grow stronger: a sufficiently capable model will recognise and pass any test we can construct.

Limited-parameter finetuning on misalignment data can both distinguish a latently misaligned model from an aligned one and identify the form of misalignment, even without an aligned control. The method sidesteps concerns of eval awareness by measuring how well the model learns to be misaligned rather than whether it is currently misaligned.

Validated on sleeper agents, contextual sleeper agents, and AuditBench models (Sheshadri et al., 2026).

6 Next Steps
